## 1. New York University (NYU)

### Types of Aid Available
NYU graduate students can access four main categories of financial aid:
- **Scholarships and Grants** (institutional, merit- and need-based)
- **Federal Work-Study / Student Employment** (on-campus and off-campus work opportunities)
- **Federal Loans** (Direct Unsubsidized Loans and Graduate PLUS Loans)
- **Private/Alternative Loans**

### PhD Stipend Amounts
NYU's central financial aid pages do not publish a universal PhD stipend figure — stipend amounts are set at the **school/department level** (e.g., the Graduate School of Arts & Science, Tandon School of Engineering, etc.). Students funded through teaching or research assistantships typically receive a tuition remission plus a living stipend, but the exact dollar amount varies by program.

### Master's Funding
Master's students at NYU are largely **self-funded**. Institutional grants and scholarships exist but are limited and competitive. Most master's students rely on federal loans, private loans, and any departmental merit awards. NYU's aid office explicitly directs graduate/professional students to their individual school's financial aid resources, indicating that centralized funding is minimal for master's programs.

### Named Fellowships & Competitive Awards
NYU's central aid pages reference institutional grants and fellowships broadly but do not list specific named fellowships on the general financial aid portal. Named fellowships and competitive awards are administered **at the school or department level** (e.g., MacCracken Fellowships through GSAS for PhD students, which cover full tuition and a stipend).

### Requirements to Be Considered for Aid
- Submit the **FAFSA** (Free Application for Federal Student Aid) — required for federal loans, Work-Study, and need-based institutional aid
- Apply for admission — aid consideration is typically part of the admissions process for doctoral students
- Maintain satisfactory academic progress (SAP)
- U.S. citizenship or eligible non-citizen status for federal programs; international students are limited to institutional and private aid options
- Enroll at least half-time for most federal aid programs

## 2. Columbia University

### Types of Aid Available
Columbia offers a broad spectrum of graduate funding, particularly through its engineering and arts & sciences graduate schools:
- **Institutional Grants** (need- and merit-based, awarded by academic departments)
- **Fellowships** (competitive, awarded at admission or through department nomination)
- **Teaching Assistantships (TAs)** — course credit, tuition remission, and stipend
- **Research Assistantships (RAs)** — funded by faculty research grants
- **On-Campus and Off-Campus Employment**
- **Federal Loans** (FAFSA-based: Direct Unsubsidized and Graduate PLUS)
- **Private/Alternative Loans**
- **Veterans Benefits / GI Bill**
- **External/Outside Fellowships** (NSF, Fulbright, etc.)

### PhD Stipend Amounts
Columbia's Bulletin indicates PhD students funded through fellowships or assistantships receive a package typically covering **full tuition remission + health insurance + a living stipend**. Specific stipend dollar amounts are set at the department level and vary; Columbia Engineering's bulletin references competitive multi-year funding packages for PhD students, though exact figures are not centrally published on the general aid page.

### Master's Funding
Master's students at Columbia are predominantly **self-funded**. Institutional grants for master's students are limited; most master's students rely on federal loans, private loans, or employer tuition benefits. Some departments offer partial merit scholarships or graduate assistantships to master's students, but full funding is rare. The Bulletin notes that questions about institutional grants and assistantships should be directed to the **academic department**, underscoring the decentralized nature of master's funding.

### Named Fellowships & Competitive Awards
Columbia references several fellowship categories:
- **Departmental Fellowships** — awarded at the point of PhD admission, covering tuition + stipend
- **External Awards** — Columbia actively encourages students to seek NSF Graduate Research Fellowships, Fulbright Awards, Ford Foundation Fellowships, and similar nationally competitive awards
- **University Fellowships** — select university-wide competitive fellowships exist for top incoming doctoral students
- Specific named fellowships (e.g., Presidential Fellowships) are administered at the school level and vary by division

### Requirements to Be Considered for Aid
- Submit the **FAFSA** by the priority deadline (required for federal aid and considered for need-based institutional aid)
- Apply for departmental funding **simultaneously with the admissions application**; the key deadline noted is **December 15** for departmental fellowship and assistantship consideration
- Admission to a degree program is a prerequisite
- Satisfactory Academic Progress (SAP) required to maintain aid
- International students are eligible for institutional fellowships and assistantships but not federal aid programs

## 3. Cornell Tech

### Types of Aid Available
Cornell Tech's financial aid is notably more **limited and market-oriented** compared to NYU and Columbia, reflecting its professional-school focus:
- **Merit Scholarships** (limited, awarded at admission for select master's programs)
- **Cornell University Financial Aid** (federal loans via Cornell's main financial aid office)
- **Federal Loans** (Direct Unsubsidized and Graduate PLUS, for eligible U.S. students)
- **Private/Alternative Loans**
- **Employer Sponsorship** (especially common for Johnson Cornell Tech MBA students)
- **Johnson's Financial Planning resources** for MBA students

### PhD Stipend Amounts
Cornell Tech itself is primarily a **professional master's and MBA campus** — it does not host a traditional standalone PhD program on Roosevelt Island. PhD students affiliated with Cornell Tech are enrolled through Cornell University's Graduate School (Ithaca-based) and receive funding through the standard Cornell Graduate School model, which includes **full tuition + health insurance + a living stipend/fellowship** (the Cornell Graduate School publishes base stipend rates, e.g., approximately **$34,000–$40,000/year** for 2024–2025 depending on field and year of study).

### Master's Funding
Master's students at Cornell Tech are overwhelmingly **self-funded**. The tuition rates are very high:
- **Master of Engineering (MEng):** $73,946/year (2026–2027)
- **Jacobs Technion-Cornell Dual MS:** $73,946/year (first-year costs only)
- **Johnson Cornell Tech MBA:** $139,030/year
- **Master of Laws (LLM):** $87,908/year

These figures highlight that Cornell Tech's master's programs are priced as **professional/industry-track degrees**. Limited merit scholarships exist but do not typically cover full costs. Students are expected to finance education through loans, savings, or employer sponsorship.

### Named Fellowships & Competitive Awards
Cornell Tech's tuition page does not prominently feature named institutional fellowships for master's students. However:
- Students may compete for **university-wide Cornell fellowships** through the Ithaca graduate school
- MBA students have access to **Johnson School scholarships and financial planning** resources
- External fellowships (NSF, etc.) are applicable for research-track students
- The **Jacobs Technion-Cornell program** is itself a distinctive dual-degree opportunity, though not a funded fellowship

### Requirements to Be Considered for Aid
- Admission to a Cornell Tech degree program
- For federal loans: FAFSA submission through Cornell University's Financial Aid Office
- For merit scholarships: consideration is typically automatic at the time of application — no separate scholarship application required in most programs
- MBA applicants may apply for Johnson School scholarships as part of the MBA application
- Employer sponsorship arrangements are handled independently between the student and employer
